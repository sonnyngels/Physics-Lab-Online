Chapter 2: Solid, Liquid, Gases

2.1 States of Matter

Matter exists in three main states: solid, liquid, and gas. They differ by particle arrangement, movement, and properties.

Property Solid Liquid Gas
Shape Definite Takes shape of container No fixed shape
Volume Fixed Fixed Variable
Particle Arrangement Closely packed Close but can slide Far apart
Particle Movement Vibrate in place Slide past each other Move freely
Compressibility Almost none Slight High
Example Ice, metal Water, oil Air, oxygen

2.2 Density

Density is the mass of a substance per unit volume. It helps explain why some objects float while others sink.

ρ = m / V

  • ρ = density
  • m = mass
  • V = volume

Solids usually have higher density, liquids medium, and gases very low density because of particle spacing. An object floats if it is less dense than the fluid it is in.

2.3 Pressure

Pressure is the force applied per area. It shows how strongly something pushes on a surface.

P = F / A

  • P = pressure
  • F = force
  • A = area

Pressure increases with greater force or smaller area. In fluids, pressure comes from the weight of particles above a point.

2.4 Hydrostatic Pressure

Hydrostatic pressure is the pressure in a fluid at rest due to the weight of the fluid above a point.

P = ρgh

  • P = hydrostatic pressure
  • ρ = density of fluid
  • g = gravity
  • h = depth

It increases with depth. For example, divers feel more pressure deeper underwater, and the bottom of a dam experiences higher pressure than the top.

2.5 Pascal’s Principle

When pressure is applied to a fluid in a closed container, the pressure spreads equally in all directions. Liquids are nearly incompressible, so this principle allows pressure to move objects far from the applied point.

Pascal’s Principle is used in hydraulic machines such as:

  • Hydraulic jacks
  • Car brakes
  • Hydraulic lifts
  • Construction machinery

2.1 Heat Capacity and Specific Heat Capacity

Heat capacity (C) is the amount of heat energy needed to raise the temperature of an object by 1°C or 1 K.

C = Q / △T

Where:

  • Q = heat energy (J)
  • ΔT = temperature change

Specific heat capacity (c) is the heat needed to raise the temperature of 1 kg of a substance by 1°C (or 1 K).

Q = mc△T

Where:

  • Q = heat energy (J)
  • m = mass (kg)
  • c = specific heat capacity (J/kg°C)
  • ΔT = temperature change (°C)

Example: Water has a high specific heat capacity (~4186 J/kg°C), meaning it needs a lot of heat to increase its temperature.

2.2 Specific Latent Heat (Melting and Boiling)

Latent heat is heat energy absorbed or released when a substance changes its state without changing temperature.

Types:

1. Latent Heat of Fusion (Melting)

Heat needed to change a substance from solid → liquid.

Example: Ice → Water

2. Latent Heat of Vaporization (Boiling)

Heat needed to change a substance from liquid → gas.

Example: Water → Steam.

Q = mL

Where:

  • Q = heat energy (J)
  • m = mass (kg)
  • L = specific latent heat (J/kg)

During a phase change, temperature stays constant because the heat energy is used to break intermolecular bonds, not increase temperature.

2.3 The Difference Between Boiling and Evaporation

Boiling Evaporation
Happens at a fixed temperature (boiling point) Happens at any temperature
Occurs throughout the liquid Occurs only at the surface
Fast process Slow process
Produces bubbles No bubbles

2.4 Heating and Cooling Curve Graph

A heating curve shows how temperature changes as heat is added to a substance.

Stages:

  • Solid heating → temperature increases
  • Melting point → temperature stays constant
  • Liquid heating → temperature increases
  • Boiling point → temperature stays constant
  • Gas heating → temperature increases

Flat sections of the graph represent phase change (latent heat).

2.5 Black’s Principle

Black’s Principle: When hot and cold objects are mixed, heat lost by the hot object equals heat gained by the cold object.

Q(lost) = Q(gained)

Example: Hot water + cold water → reach thermal equilibrium.

This principle is used in calorimetry experiments.

2.6 Heat Transfer

Heat always flows from higher temperature to lower temperature.

1. Conduction

Heat transfer through direct contact between particles.

Example: Metal spoon in hot soup becomes hot.

2. Convection

Heat transfer through movement of fluids (liquid or gas).

Example: Boiling water circulation and sea breeze.

3. Radiation

Heat transfer through electromagnetic waves (no medium needed).

Example: Heat from the Sun reaching Earth.

2.7 Consequences and Applications of Energy Transfer

Examples in daily life:

  • Conduction – Cooking pans made of metal, ironing clothes
  • Convection – Hot air balloons rise, ocean currents
  • Radiation – Solar panels, feeling heat from a fire

Applications help improve energy efficiency and technology.
